Abstract

ManiFeel, a reproducible and scalable simulation benchmark for studying supervised visuotactile manipulation policies across a diverse set of tasks and scenarios. ManiFeel presents a comprehensive benchmark suite spanning a diverse set of manipulation tasks, evaluating various policies, input modalities, and tactile representation methods. Through extensive experiments, our analysis reveals key factors that influence supervised visuotactile policy learning, identifies the types of tasks where tactile sensing is most beneficial, and highlights promising directions for future research in visuotactile policy learning. ManiFeel aims to establish a reproducible benchmark for supervised visuotactile policy learning, supporting progress in visuotactile manipulation and perception. To facilitate future research and ensure reproducibility, we will release our codebase, datasets, training logs, and pretrained checkpoints.

Reactive Experiments

We evaluate visuotactile and vision-only policies in a real-world tactile sorting task, where the robot must differentiate between two visually similar objects—a table tennis ball and a golf ball—and place them in the yellow and red bowls, respectively.

During roll-outs with human disturbances, the visuotactile policy shows robust tactile reactivity. When the grasped ball is removed, the robot actively searches for it and continues the task.

In contrast, the vision-only policy lacks this adaptive behavior and fails to recover from such interruptions.

Task and Environment Design

We design a suite of diverse manipulation tasks to systematically evaluate supervised visuotactile policy learning under varying sensory conditions. The tasks are grouped into six categories, reflecting different levels of reliance on visual and tactile modalities.

Tactile-based Manipulation Tasks

The following tasks require significant tactile sensing for successful manipulation. These examples demonstrate the importance of tactile feedback in challenging scenarios where vision alone is insufficient.

Blind Insertion (Tactile)

Tactile Exploration (Tactile)

Tactile Sorting (Visuotactile)

Vision-based Manipulation Tasks

These tasks are primarily solved using visual feedback. They demonstrate scenarios where vision is sufficient for successful manipulation, highlighting the strengths and limitations of vision-only policies.

Blind Insertion (Vision)

Tactile Exploration (Vision)

Tactile Sorting (Vision)

Contact-rich Manipulation Tasks

These tasks involve complex, contact-rich interactions that require both visual and tactile feedback for successful completion. They showcase the advantages of visuotactile policies in challenging manipulation scenarios.

Gear Assembly (Visuotactile)

Power Plug (Visuotactile)

USB Insertion (Visuotactile)
